Part 01 · Indexes

An index is a scoreboard.

An index doesn't do anything. It's just a number that tracks a basket of companies, so people can answer the question: "How is the market doing today?"

When the news says "the market was up 1.2% today", they don't mean every stock on Earth. They mean one specific index — usually the S&P 500.

Think of it like a sports league
The S&P 500 is the league table of the top 500 U.S. companies. The FTSE 100 is the top 100 in the UK. The number rises and falls based on how those companies' shares are doing.

You can't buy "an index" directly — it's just a measurement. But you can buy something that tracks an index, which is where ETFs come in (we'll get there).

Part 02 · Bonds

Not all loans are equal.

From Lesson 1: a bond is a loan. But who you're lending to massively changes the deal. A loan to the U.S. government is one thing. A loan to a struggling startup is another.

Tap each tab to see the main types — from safest to riskiest.

Government Bonds · Safest

Lending to a country.

The borrower is a national government — the U.S., the UK, etc. They print the money they pay you back with, so the chance of default is very low.

You'll hear these called Treasuries (U.S.), Gilts (UK), or Bunds (Germany).

Risk: very low Return: low Use: safety
Municipal Bonds · Safer

Lending to a city or state.

The borrower is a city, county, or state — funding things like schools, roads, or hospitals. Slightly riskier than national bonds but often tax-free in the U.S.

Mostly a U.S. concept. The UK equivalent is rarer.

Risk: low Return: low-med Use: tax efficiency
Corporate Bonds · Medium

Lending to a company.

The borrower is a company — Apple, Coca-Cola, Tesco. They pay you more interest than governments do, because they can go bankrupt.

"Investment grade" means rated safe by agencies like Moody's or S&P. These are solid, established businesses.

Risk: medium Return: medium Use: steady income
High-Yield (a.k.a. "Junk") · Riskiest

Lending to risky borrowers.

The borrower is a company with weaker finances — startups, turnarounds, heavily indebted businesses. They have to offer high interest to convince anyone to lend.

"Junk" is the unflattering nickname. The official term is high-yield. More potential return, more potential disaster.

Risk: high Return: high Use: yield hunting

Part 03 · ETFs

ETFs are baskets.

An ETF (Exchange-Traded Fund) is a single product that holds dozens or thousands of investments inside it. You buy one share of the ETF — and you instantly own a tiny slice of everything inside.

Part 03 · Flavors

ETFs for nearly anything.

The most common types he'll see:

Index ETFs
Track an index like the S&P 500. You buy one share, you own a piece of all 500 companies. Examples: VOO, SPY (S&P 500), VUSA (S&P 500 for UK investors).
Bond ETFs
Hold a bundle of bonds — government, corporate, or both. Example: BND holds thousands of U.S. bonds.
Sector ETFs
Focus on one industry: tech, healthcare, energy, etc. Riskier because you're not diversified across the whole economy.
Country / Region ETFs
Track foreign markets. ISF tracks the FTSE 100. VWRL tracks the whole world's stock markets in one product.
The simplest beginner strategy in existence: buy one global index ETF every month. Done.
