Recessions are normal. The market is moody.

S&P 500 · stylized · 100 yrs Red bands = recessions. The line keeps climbing.

The economy rises and falls — it has been doing this for as long as we've kept records. Recessions feel scary, but they are scheduled visitors, not surprises.

This lesson covers two big ideas: recession cycles, and Benjamin Graham's Mr. Market — a framework that turns market panic into your advantage.

Part 01 · The Record

The economy has done this ~34 times.

According to the National Bureau of Economic Research — the official scorekeeper — the U.S. has had 34 recessions since 1857. That's roughly one every 5 years.

Part 03 · The Ratio

The good times outlast the bad.

Recessions are loud, but they're a sliver of history. Since World War II, the U.S. economy has spent ~85% of months expanding — and only ~15% in recession.

Months in expansion vs recession · 1945-today
85% · Growth
15%
The economy is almost always growing. Recessions are the interruptions, not the rule.
Why this matters
If you panic-sell during the red bars, you miss the green years that follow — and the green years are where most of the wealth gets built.

Part 04 · After The Storm

The market always recovers.

"Always" is a strong word — but it's been true through wars, depressions, oil crises, and pandemics. Markets fall hard during recessions, then climb back to new highs.

S&P 500 — falls & recoveries

Great Depression (1929)-86% → recovered
1973 oil crisis-48% → recovered
Dot-com bust (2000)-49% → recovered
Global Financial Crisis (2008)-57% → recovered

Every single one of these felt like the world was ending. Every single one became, in hindsight, a buying opportunity.

Part 05 · The Framework

Meet Mr. Market.

In 1949, Benjamin Graham — Warren Buffett's teacher — wrote a book called The Intelligent Investor. In it, he introduced one of the best metaphors in all of finance.

"I'll buy or sell. Your call."
— Calm

Imagine you co-own a business with a man named Mr. Market. Every single day, he shows up at your door and offers either to buy your share from you, or to sell you his.

The catch: he is emotionally unstable. Some days he's euphoric and quotes you absurdly high prices. Other days he's depressed and offers to sell for pennies.

Part 07 · The Takeaway

Recessions serve the patient investor.

Put the two ideas together:

1. Recessions are part of the deal
They've happened 34+ times. They will happen again. Expect them the way you expect winter.
2. They make Mr. Market panic
During a recession, Mr. Market dumps quality businesses at fire-sale prices. He's not analyzing — he's afraid.
3. That's when great investors buy
If a business is still going to be a good business in 10 years, a 40% discount on its shares isn't a tragedy — it's a sale.
"Be fearful when others are greedy, and greedy when others are fearful." — Warren Buffett

Three ideas to remember for the rest of your investing life:

Recessions are cycles, not catastrophes
They've happened ~34 times. They'll happen ~12 more times in your life. This is fine.
Mr. Market works for you, not the other way around
You never have to act on his mood. Use his panic, ignore his euphoria.
A crash is a sale
If you were going to buy a quality business at $100, you should be thrilled to buy it at $60.
