When people talk about investing, they almost always mean one of two things: buying a piece of a company or lending money out for interest.

That's it. Stocks and bonds. Tap Next to see how each one actually works.

Class 01 · Equities

Stocks are ownership.

A share of stock is a tiny slice of a real company. If you own one share of Apple, you literally own a piece of Apple — its factories, its products, its profits.

You

You buy a slice · The company grows · Your slice is worth more
How you make money
The stock price rises as the company grows, or the company pays you a share of its profits (a dividend).
The catch
No promises. If the business struggles, your slice is worth less. Stocks swing — sometimes wildly.
Best for
Long-term growth. Historically, stocks return ~7-10% per year on average — but with bumps.

Class 02 · Fixed Income

Bonds are loans.

A bond is an IOU. You hand money to a government or a company, and they promise to pay it back — plus interest — on a set date.

You
→ $ →
🏛️

You
You lend money · They pay regular interest · You get your money back at the end
How you make money
Fixed interest payments (called the coupon) on a schedule, plus your original loan returned at the end.
The catch
Lower returns than stocks. And if the borrower goes bankrupt, you might not get paid back.
Best for
Predictability and protection. Historically, bonds return ~3-5% per year — steadier, gentler.

The two big ideas to walk away with:

Stocks = ownership
Higher possible returns, bumpier ride.
Bonds = lending
Lower returns, steadier payments.

Everything else in investing — ETFs, mutual funds, index funds, 401(k)s — is mostly just bundles of these two things.
